# THE WORKSHOP — Visual Development Programme

**Status:** Working programme for staged implementation

## Governing Experience

THE WORKSHOP should produce **wondering and wandering awe**. It is a private working institution that the visitor has somehow been permitted to enter. Its sense of scale comes from coherent architecture, withheld access and real departments at work—not effects, generic game systems or a catalogue of decorated rooms.

The visitor receives one strong central invitation and several peripheral mysteries.

## Institutional Grammar

- Former industrial workshop: painted steel, timber, old wired/ribbed glass, practical construction and visible repair.
- Cool daylight and workshop fluorescents establish the public working atmosphere; warmth is reserved for local human or artistic interventions.
- Rooms are different departments of one private institution, not radically unrelated worlds.
- Signage is practical, intermittent and slightly withheld.
- Visual character comes from depth, material, work traces and the room beyond—not neon frames, portal fields, floor markers or permanent HUD language.

## Release 1 — Entry Rite and Chaperone

**Purpose:** Replace the current immediate Hall spectacle with a credible rite of arrival.

- Create a shallow ascent to a tall internal steel-and-glass threshold.
- The visitor crosses into the actual Workshop only after that ascent.
- Keep the first light cool and functional.
- Replace the current toy-like Grove/Hall bust with a monumental, credible HrM relief-bust derived from verified recurring face works, including the *cadavre expat* assets.
- The chaperone sits slightly off-axis and is discovered after entry.
- It is mostly silent and monumental. On approach, it offers either concise orientation or a deadpan-absurdist institutional remark.
- Its mouth behaves as a subtle sculptural aperture only when speaking; no cartoon facial animation or NPC performance.

**Acceptance:** from entry the building, not a character or screen, pulls the visitor forward; the bust is credible as art and architecture before it is understood as interactive.

## Release 2 — Hood as Visible Atelier

**Purpose:** Merge Hood/Headquarters with the main Workshop rather than treating it as a separate destination.

- Make the Hood visible from the main shared space through industrial glass.
- Establish it as an active painting-and-collage atelier: long worktables, stacked/drying panels, pigment traces, cutting surfaces, drawers, ladders, work lamps and selective verified HrM works-in-progress.
- Avoid generic artist-studio clutter, stock props and an inert decorative office.
- The atelier is initially visible but not immediately accessible.
- Its entrance is found through ordinary spatial exploration, not a quest or permission gate.
- Once inside, visitors can inspect selected works-in-progress.

**Acceptance:** the visitor sees a credible working department before they can enter it, and the spatial route to it can be discovered naturally.

## Release 3 — Hall Re-composition

**Purpose:** Protect the forward invitation and make rooms discoveries.

- Recompose the Hall around the entry threshold, forward architectural event and the glass-visible atelier.
- All side rooms recede from the entry axis and reveal themselves after a lateral choice.
- Use continuous wall planes, recesses, dark leaves, inward-facing plaques and interior-led light.
- Preserve portal destinations, hitboxes, travel targets, interaction and accessibility behaviour.

**Acceptance:** no room title, coloured perimeter, field or floor marker competes with the entry sequence; the Hall feels larger because its departments are withheld.

## Release 4 — Institutional Graphics and Wayfinding

**Purpose:** Make the building legible without turning it into a visitor centre.

- Establish one typography, plaque, caption, map and room-marker system based on practical institutional remnants.
- Labels should be discovered at thresholds, desks, drawers and wall returns rather than broadcast across rooms.
- The minimap and UI must become quieter, more spatially coherent and secondary to the world.

**Acceptance:** visitors can orient themselves when they seek information, but visual guidance does not pre-empt discovery.

## Release 5 — Department Passes

**Purpose:** Give major rooms distinct programmes while retaining the shared institutional grammar.

Priority order after Releases 1–4:

1. Studio — functioning listening/recording department, practical rather than decorative.
2. Venue — private screening department with credible cinema scale and one dominant screen.
3. Thinking Room — Art Deco research department with a real desk, screen and limited art.
4. Experimental Garden — garden/projection/window department with designed, spatial media.
5. Remaining rooms — each justified by programme, not merely visual variety.

**Acceptance:** each department is memorable from what it permits, how it is lit and how it is discovered—not from a different themed palette.

## Release 6 — Material, Media and Performance Discipline

**Purpose:** Turn accumulated work into a stable visual experience.

- Correct screen depth/occlusion, windows, artworks, captions, door clearances and furniture placement.
- Remove inert, repetitive or game-like visual cues.
- Profile heavy rooms, especially the Hood, before adding visual density.
- Review desktop and narrow mobile viewports after each substantial release.

## Delivery Cadence

Each release follows the same sequence:

1. A one-page spatial/visual brief approved by Pedro.
2. An isolated branch from freshly merged `main`.
3. One narrowly scoped implementation plan.
4. Desktop and mobile captures plus interaction verification.
5. Pedro’s aesthetic review before a PR is marked ready.
6. A review-only PR; merge/deployment remain subject to the five direct yes/no confirmations.

No release begins from an unmerged visual branch. No visual change is called complete merely because tests pass.
